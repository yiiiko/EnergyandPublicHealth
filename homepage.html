The Hidden Cost of Energy



We rely on electricity for almost everything we use in homes, schools, businesses, and factories.

In the U.S. the electricity we use is generated in fossil fuel power plants such as coal and natural gas. These power plants emit air pollutants and greenhouse gases (GHGs). Air pollution impacts the lives of millions of people; GHGs drive climate change.

What are the human health and environmental impacts of air pollutants?

Breathing in air pollutants impact our respiratory, cardiovascular and immune systems. Studies indicate that almost every organ in our bodies can be impacted by air pollution. In 2013, the World Health Organization classified air pollutants as carcinogenic to humans. Air pollutants are responsible for acid deposition which impact ecosystems and damage to infrastructure. Air pollutants also weaken the immune systems of plants and animals and lower agricultural productivity.

What are the human health and environmental impacts of greenhouse gases (GHGs)?

GHGs in Earth’s atmosphere contribute to the greenhouse effect. As levels of GHGs increase, the average surface temperature of Earth increases which in turn drives climate change. Impacts of climate change include rising sea-levels, extreme weather events, drought, spread of diseases, and driving the extinction of species.

Hidden Cost of Energy: Air Pollutants and GHGs

Economists monetize the health and environmental impacts of emissions of air pollutants and GHGs from power plants as an external cost. The external cost is the “hidden” cost of the electricity we use in our homes, schools, businesses as this cost is not reflected in what we pay for in our electricity bills.

This external cost results from the impacts of air pollution and climate change such as hospital bills, pay loss due to sick days, emergency room visits, higher death rates, extreme weather events, loss of agriculture from drought, and loss of property from wildfires. This external cost is a cost borne by society and so is also considered a “social” cost often paid for from our taxes.

These “costs” could be shifted towards “savings” by transitioning towards “cleaner” energy sources. Money saved by lower health costs, less damage from hurricanes, wildfires, and sea level rise could be used to invest in cleaner energy sources, education, healthcare, etc.

Comparing Electrical Energy Sources

To compare electrical energy sources, we will use a life cycle analysis approach. The health and environmental impacts of an energy source are assessed not just when it is generating electricity but also the impacts associated with extraction of raw materials, construction of the power plant, running the power plant, and dismantling the power plant.

These are not the only metrics of comparison but will allow us to objectively evaluate each energy source. These are also metrics often debated by policy makes and in the news media. What is the role of environmental regulations in lowering emissions of air pollutants and GHGs?
Do these regulations work? Are they cost-effective?

Public outcry triggered by smog in cities like Donora, Los Angeles, and New York City, where people could “feel” and “taste” the air they were breathing in, triggered Congressional action and the passing of the 1970 Clean Air Act (CAA) . This Act, and subsequent amendments, regulates the levels of six air pollutants . Since the passing of the Clean Air Act the air quality in the U.S. has improved.

As can be seen below, the levels of air pollutants - sulfur dioxide (SO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx) and particulate matter (PM2.5, PM10) have all been reduced. A major source for all of these air pollutants are electrical power plants.

While the air in the U.S. is “cleaner” today compared to 1970, over 100 million people in the U.S. live in areas which struggle to meet one or more of these mandatory air quality standards. Cities in states like California and Texas still struggle with smog.
It is also important to note that while levels of air pollutants have decrease because of the Clean Air Act, levels of GHGs have not declined. Some states have adopted targets to reduce emissions of GHGs.
Imagine if there are federal limits to emissions of GHGs. How much more money could be saved by lowering the hidden costs of GHG emissions?

Q: Are the “cost” of regulations higher than the “benefits”?

Environmental regulations are often presented as being expensive and whether or not the "benefits" of regulations outweigh the "costs". In the case of the CAA the answer is clear - the benefits far outweighs the costs. And the beneficiaries are the people who reside in the U.S.

As a result of the passing of the 1970 CAA and amendments, the air quality in the U.S. has improved, significantly lowering the “external cost” as a result of a lowering of the negative impacts on human and ecosystem health from air pollution. Some studies suggest for every $1 invested in regulations to improve air quality, $40 was returned to the U.S. economy, saving trillions of dollars since the passing of the Clean Air Act in 1970.
